    14→102:What are my maintenance calories?
    15→104:Your maintenance calories are the number of calories you need to maintain your current weight, without losing or gaining weight. This number is sometimes called your total daily energy expenditure (TDEE), since if you eat the same number of calories you burn (expend), you will maintain weight. You can estimate your maintenance calories using the Mifflin-St Jeor formula, multiplying by the Standard Activity Factor (SAF) which best reflects your activity level.
    16→106:How many calories should I eat per day?
    17→108:The number of calories you should eat depends on your weight, height, age and gender. For a woman of healthy weight, eating 2,000 calories per day is generally recommended, while men should aim for 2,500 calories. If you are aiming to lose weight, you can eat fewer calories or increase your level of physical activity. Most people should not eat less than 1,200 calories per day if you are a woman and 1,500 calories per day if you are a man.
    18→110:Low and Very Low Calorie Diets (VLCD), which are below 1,200 calories per day and 800 calories per day respectively, are only suitable under medical supervision and where there is a clinical rationale for rapid weight loss. 
    19→112:You can use Gym Geek's calorie calculator to estimate your maintenance calories and calculate a calorie deficit based on your weight loss goal.
    20→114:How many calories should I eat to lose weight?
    21→116:To lose weight, you should maintain a calorie deficit below your total daily energy expenditure (TDEE). The number of calories you should eat depends on your current weight, height, age and gender, as well as your activity level. A calorie deficit of 500 calories per day will typically allow you to lose up to one pound per week, and 1,000 calories per day will see you lose up to two pounds per week.
    22→118:Most people should not attempt to lose more than 2 lb per week without medical supervision. After deducting your calorie deficit, your daily calorie goal should be not be less than the minimum calorie intake (1,200 calories per day for women and 1,500 calories per day for men). Eating less than the minimum calorie intake is only suitable with medical supervision.
    23→120:How do I calculate my calorie deficit?
    24→122:First, you will need to calculate your Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR). This is the number of calories you burn at rest, but it doesn't include the calories you burn through exercise or the digestion of food. Multiply this number by your activity level, measured with the Standard Activity Factor (SAF) (also known as Physical Activity Level or PAL) to get your TDEE. 
    25→124:Finally, subtract between 250 and 1,000 calories from your TDEE to lose between 0.5 lb and 2 lb per week. Gym Geek recommends checking your calorie deficit using an online calorie calculator.
    26→126:How many calories do women need to lose weight?
    27→128:The calorie deficit for a woman losing weight will depend on her age, height, current weight and activity level. Because women tend to have carry less fat-free mass (muscle) than men, they generally need to eat fewer calories than men to lose weight. This is because muscle is more metabolically active that fat tissue.
    28→130:To lose 1 lb per week, an overweight woman of average height might eat 1,300 calories per day if she was sedentary, and up to 1,800 calories per day if she was moderately active. A woman should never eat fewer than 1,200 calories per day unless instructed and medically supervised by a doctor.
    29→132:How many calories do men need to lose weight?

    62→229:2. TDEE
    63→231:Once we have an estimate of your BMR, we then calculate your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) estimate. TDEE is a measure of how many calories you burn per day, adding together your BMR along with the calories you burn through physical activity and the calories you burn while digesting food (thermic effect).
    64→233:Because TDEE takes into account your activity level (i.e. whether you are sedentary, lightly active, moderately active, etc), it's a more accurate estimate of your daily calorie burn.
    65→238:In your calorie calculator results, the first number you see is your TDEE. Your TDEE is shown in kcal (kilocalories) but you can switch to kJ (kilojoules) if you prefer. One calorie (1 kcal) is equivalent to 4.184 kilojoules (4.184 kJ).

    69→250:How is BMR calculated?
    70→252:BMR, or Basal Metabolic Rate, is the number of calories your body needs at total rest to power your most basic functions. This includes the baseline activity of your organs, but does not include the digestion of food. It also doesn’t include any calories you need for physical activity and exercise.
    71→257:BMR typically increases with your weight and height, because you have a larger body mass to support. However, you can expect your BMR to decrease over time as you age. This is because most people lose muscle mass and gain fat as they age.
    72→259:A number of formulas for estimating BMR have been created over time. These formulas are produced by statistical studies that look at representative populations, measuring their BMR and crunching the data to build accurate formulas.
    73→261:Our calorie calculator uses the Mifflin-St Jeor equation, which came out of a 1990 study of 498 people across both genders, all adult age groups and normal weight and obese persons. The study introduced a formula based on a person’s weight, height, age and gender.
    74→263:For men, the formula is 9.99 x Weight + 6.25 x Height – 4.92 x Age + 5.
    75→265:For women, the formula is 9.99 x Weight + 6.25 x Height – 4.92 x Age – 161.
    76→267:Sometimes, you may see these formulas simplified to 10 x Weight + 6.25 x Height - 5 x Age + 5 for men and 10 x Weight + 6.25 x Height - 5 x Age - 161 for women. These minor differences in rounding make only a tiny difference in the final result.
    77→269:Is Mifflin-St Jeor accurate?
    78→271:BMR calculators using Mifflin-St Jeor are fairly accurate for most people. The formula, however, does not account for specific factors like muscle mass. Specifically, if you have a high muscle mass, you may find that Mifflin-St Jeor underestimates your BMR. This is because muscle burns more calories than fat cells at rest.
    79→273:A study published in the Journal of the American Dietetic Association concluded that the Mifflin-St Jeor equation was more likely to estimate calorie needs to within 10% compared to other equations.
    80→278:How are maintenance calories adjusted for weight loss and gain?
    81→280:When it comes to losing or gaining weight, creating a calorie deficit or surplus relative to your maintenance calories is key to achieving your goal. There are a number of ways to adjust your calories to reflect a weight loss or weight gain goal.
    82→285:A popular rule-of-thumb relies on the idea that 1 lb (0.45 kg) of fat is equivalent to 3,500 calories. Many people therefore believe that maintaining a calorie deficit of 500 calories per day (3,500 divided by 7) will allow you to lose 1 lb per week. You can use this idea to adjust the deficit or excess up and down to evaluate the impact it will have on your weight loss or weight gain goal.
    83→287:In reality, weight loss is not that simple and it is impossible to estimate an accurate deficit or excess for most people. This rule has some disadvantages and may not be accurate for long-term weight loss plans. However, it is a reasonable starting point for many people.
    84→289:Our calorie calculator uses this principle to produce its estimates. Once you have started your weight plan, you should review your progress after 3-4 weeks and adjust your calorie intake accordingly.
    85→293:Calories in, calories out (CICO) is the key concept behind any weight loss or weight gain strategy. The idea is that any weight loss or gain is determined by the balance of calories eaten ("calories in") against how many calories you burn ("calories out", or "total daily energy expenditure").
    86→295:The easiest way to create a calorie deficit is on the calories in side of the equation, i.e. by eating fewer calories each day. But it’s also possible to create a calorie deficit with the calories out side of CICO. This is possible because, although you can do little to change your BMR, you do have total control over your physical activity.
    87→297:Doing exercise burns calories, and the more you do the more calories get added onto your daily energy expenditure. Since a calorie deficit is just the difference between calories in and calories out, this allows you to create a deficit simply by doing more exercise.
    88→302:Even a small amount of exercise can have a big impact on your daily energy expenditure. If you burn an extra 100 calories per day, over a week you will have burned 700 calories. That’s equivalent to 0.2 pounds of extra weight lost. Compound this over months and it starts to have a real impact on your weight loss journey.
    89→304:Our 100 calorie fat burners are small amounts of exercise or physical activity that burn 100 calories in a short amount of time. Doing these exercises every day can increase your daily energy expenditure, making it just a little easier to stay in deficit.
    90→308:Some people argue that calorie calculators don’t work because each person has a unique metabolism. Everyone’s metabolism is different, and there are many situations that cause a person’s metabolism to higher or lower than average:
    91→312:Genetics – A person’s genetics is known to affect their metabolism. This is something that can’t be measured, so formulas like Mifflin-St Jeor effectively “average out” across all groups of people.
    92→314:Hormones – Many kinds of hormones have an impact on your body’s metabolism. Every person has slightly different levels of hormones, which can impact your metabolic rate. Conditions like hypothyroidism or hyperthyroidism can substantially decrease or increase a person’s metabolism.
    93→316:Diet – A high protein diet is associated with slightly higher TDEEs. This is because digesting proteins requires more energy than digesting carbs or fats.
    94→318:But just because you have a lower or higher metabolism than average doesn’t mean that calorie deficit diets don't work. You just need to account for this in the estimate of your maintenance calories. If your metabolism is substantially different to average, you’ll need to adjust your maintenance calories up or down as appropriate.
    95→320:If you have a slower metabolism, this means reducing your TDEE estimate. Because of this, you’ll also need to reduce your calories in to maintain the same deficit. In other words, you’ll need to eat less food than if you had an average metabolism.
    96→322:It's not easy to measure your metabolism or maintenance calories directly, which is why most people rely on simple calorie calculators or apps that use a formula to make an estimate.

    99→331:Most adaptive TDEE algorithms are based on your calorie intake and bodyweight data over an extended period of time. They crunch this data to figure out a personalized and accurate number for your specific circumstances.

   106→347:Hidden calories
   107→349:For the best results, you’ll need to track every single calorie (within reason). If you’re new to calorie counting, it’s tempting to just record the ‘big items’ in your diet.    178→512:Maintaining muscle mass on a calorie deficit diet
   179→521:Losing weight while maintaining muscle needs both a calorie deficit and a sufficient amount of exercise. Aim to include both resistance training and regular cardio into your weight loss plan.
   180→523:Resistance training promotes the growth of muscle, increases metabolic rate and increases bone density.    181→528:Cardio complements this by promoting heart health, burning calories and improving your overall fitness level. Cardio exercises like running or cycling typically burn more calories during the workout itself when compared with resistance training.
