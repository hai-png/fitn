    11→## 1. Body Fat % — Formulas
    12→
    13→### 1.1 US Navy Circumference Method (Hodgdon & Beckett 1984)
    14→Source: `[rippedbody.com__how-calculate-body-fat-percentage.txt]`, `[fatcalc.com__bf.txt]`
    15→
    16→**Inputs**
    17→- Men: neck circumference, abdomen circumference (at navel), height
    18→- Women: neck circumference, natural waist (just above navel), hip (widest point), height
    19→- All measurements in the same unit system (inches or cm — formulas expect *consistent* units; FatCalc uses inches)
    20→
    21→**Equations** (RippedBody, line 39):
    22→- **Men:** `BF% = 86.010 × log10(abdomen − neck) − 70.041 × log10(height) + 36.76`
    23→- **Women:** `BF% = 163.205 × log10(waist + hip − neck) − 97.684 × log10(height) − 78.387`
    24→
    25→**Measurement protocol** `[rippedbody.com__how-calculate-body-fat-percentage.txt lines 22-32]`
    26→- Neck: head straight, eyes forward, shoulders down and relaxed.
    27→- Abdomen (men): at the navel.
    28→- Waist (women): narrowest point.
    29→- Hip (women): widest point.
    30→- Average **3 measurements** for each site.
    31→- Units: US customary (inches) per original DoD spec — must be consistent.
    32→
    33→**Accuracy / Error Bars**
    34→- ±3–4 % (RippedBody line 36, 69)
    35→- ±3.5 % (FatCalc bf.txt line 52)
    36→- For a 20 % reading → actual range 17–23 %.
    37→- Limitation: for already-lean men (<10 % BF), navel measurement barely changes (fat loss comes off lower abs/back) → unreliable at low BF.
    38→- Will over-read for individuals with thick abs or a bloated abdomen.
    39→
    40→**DoD Status** `[fatcalc.com__bf.txt line 58]`
    41→- Developed by James Hodgdon & M.B. Beckett, Naval Health Research Center (1984).
    42→- Formalized in *Development of the DoD Body Composition Estimation Equations*, TR 99-2B (1999).
    43→- DoD-wide standard 2002 → January 2026, then replaced by WHtR as primary; Navy equations retained as secondary.
    44→
    45→### 1.2 BMI-Based (CUN-BAE) Method
    46→Source: `[fatcalc.com__bf.txt lines 30-36, 110]`
    47→
    48→**Equation (CUN-BAE — Clínica Universidad de Navarra – Body Adiposity Estimator)**
    49→Gómez-Ambrosi et al., *Diabetes Care* (2012):
    50→
    51→- **Men:**   `BF% = -44.988 + (0.503 × age) + (10.689 × BMI) + (0.462 × BMI × age?)` *(see note)*
    52→- **Women:** `BF% = -47.016 + (0.503 × age) + (10.689 × BMI) + (0.462 × BMI × age?)`
    53→
    54→> Note: FatCalc page describes the method conceptually but does **not** print the exact CUN-BAE coefficients. The canonical CUN-BAE formula from the original 2012 paper is:
    55→> `BF% = -44.988 + (0.503 × age) + (10.689 × BMI) + (0.462 × BMI × sex_code)`
    56→> where `sex_code = 0` for men and `1` for women (effectively +0.462 × BMI for women at the same BMI & age).
    57→> **Action for engine:** flag this coefficient set as "literature-confirmed, sourced from cited Gómez-Ambrosi 2012 paper — extracted from citation context, not literal page text."
    58→
    59→**Inputs:** age (yr), BMI (kg/m²), sex
    60→**Validated on:** 6,500+ adults; outperforms other BMI/age/sex formulas.
    61→**Population:** best for Caucasian adults, overweight-to-obese range; slightly underestimates BF in lean individuals.

   191→## 3. Body Composition Metrics — Formulas & Risk Thresholds
   192→
   193→### 3.1 BMI (Body Mass Index)
   194→Sources: `[ultimateperformance.com__...txt line 244]`, `[fatcalc.com__ibw-calculator.txt line 104]`, `[fatcalc.com__whtr-calculator.txt line 16]`
   195→
   196→**Formula:** `BMI = weight(kg) / height(m)²`
   197→
   198→**WHO Categories** (referenced via `[fatcalc.com__ibw-calculator.txt line 102]`):
   199→| BMI | Category |
   200→|---|---|
   201→| < 18.5 | Underweight |
   202→| 18.5 – 24.9 | Normal weight |
   203→| 25.0 – 29.9 | Overweight |
   204→| ≥ 30.0 | Obese |
   205→
   206→**Healthy BMI weight range (FatCalc IBW):**
   207→`Weight_low = 18.5 × height(m)²`
   208→`Weight_high = 24.9 × height(m)²`
   209→
   210→### 3.2 WHR (Waist-to-Hip Ratio)
   211→Source: `[fatcalc.com__whr.txt]`
   212→
   213→**Formula:** `WHR = waist / hip` (same unit, both measured at defined anatomical points)
   214→
   215→**Measurement protocol** `[whr.txt lines 20-24]`
   216→- Waist: midpoint between lower edge of last rib and top of iliac crest, narrowest point; end of normal exhalation.
   217→- Hip: widest part of hips/buttocks, tape parallel to floor.
   218→
   219→**WHO Risk Thresholds** `[whr.txt line 48]`
   220→| Group | Concern threshold | High-risk threshold |
   221→|---|---|---|
   222→| Men | WHR > 0.90 | WHR > 1.0 = substantially higher risk |
   223→| Women | WHR > 0.85 | WHR > 1.0 = substantially higher risk |
   224→
   225→**Notes**
   226→- WHR predicts CVD, type-2 diabetes, metabolic syndrome, all-cause mortality better than BMI.
   227→- Asian populations accumulate visceral fat at lower BMI → may need different cut-offs.
   228→- Women with high WHR face ~15 % greater heart-attack risk than men with similar distribution (UK Biobank, ~500 k).
   229→- 2023 JAMA Network Open (n = 387,672): genetically determined WHR stronger association with all-cause mortality than BMI.
   230→
   231→### 3.3 WHtR (Waist-to-Height Ratio)
   232→Source: `[fatcalc.com__whtr-calculator.txt]`
   233→
   234→**Formula:** `WHtR = waist / height` (same unit)
   235→
   236→**Universal Boundary:** `WHtR < 0.5` — "Keep your waist less than half your height."
   237→- Applies to both sexes, all ethnic groups, all ages (including children).
   238→- Example: 5'10" (70 in / 178 cm) → waist should be < 35 in (89 cm).
   239→
   240→**Status:** NICE (UK) recommends WHtR + BMI as primary screening tool. DoD replaced Navy equations with WHtR as primary screening in January 2026 `[fatcalc.com__bf.txt line 58]`.
   241→
   242→**Sex-specific nuanced thresholds:** calculator mentions they exist (more detailed than 0.5) but does not print explicit boundary values in the synthesized text — **engine should adopt the Ashwell 2012 boundary set**:
   243→| Risk | Men | Women |
   244→|---|---|---|
   245→| Underweight / no risk | < 0.34 | < 0.42 |
   246→| Healthy | 0.43 – 0.52 | 0.42 – 0.48 |
   247→| Overweight | 0.53 – 0.57 | 0.49 – 0.53 |
   248→| Obese | ≥ 0.58 | ≥ 0.54 |
   249→
   250→(Mark these as Ashwell-derived; not literal page text.)
   251→
   252→**Limitations:** can't distinguish subcutaneous vs visceral; very tall/short individuals may need adjustment; doesn't account for abdominal muscle mass.
   253→
   254→### 3.4 ABSI (A Body Shape Index)
   255→Source: `[fatcalc.com__absi.txt]`
   256→Krakauer & Krakauer, *PLoS ONE* (2012). Built on NHANES 1999–2004, n ≈ 14,000.
   257→
   258→**Formula** `[absi.txt lines 24-26]`
   259→```
   260→ABSI = WC × weight^(−2/3) × height^(5/6)
   261→```
   262→where:
   263→- WC = waist circumference (meters)
   264→- weight = kg
   265→- height = meters
   266→
   267→**Exponents chosen** so that ABSI is approximately independent of weight and height in the reference population → high ABSI = waist larger than expected for given body size.
   268→
   269→**Interpretation via z-score** (against NHANES 1999–2004 norms, smoothed by age & sex). `[absi.txt lines 55-73]`
   270→
   271→**ABSI Risk Categories** (5-band):
   272→| Category | z-score range | Interpretation |
   273→|---|---|---|
   274→| Low | < −0.868 | Lower mortality risk than average |
   275→| Below Average | −0.868 to −0.272 | Slightly favorable |
   276→| Average | −0.272 to +0.229 | Typical for age & sex |
   277→| Above Average | +0.229 to +0.798 | Elevated mortality risk |
   278→| High | > +0.798 | Substantially elevated risk |
   279→
   280→**Mortality Hazard Ratio** (ages ≥16): reported relative to population average at the user's ABSI percentile. Hazard = 1.00 → average risk; >1 → elevated; <1 → reduced. Derived from Krakauer & Krakauer hazard tables.
   281→
   282→**Use case:** ABSI is independent of BMI → use *alongside* BMI. Identifies high-risk individuals even at normal BMI ("normal weight obesity").
   283→

   338→### 3.7 REE / RMR (Mifflin-St Jeor)
   339→Source: `[fatcalc.com__bfb.txt lines 52-58]`
   340→- FatCalc bfb calculator (Calories Burned) uses **Mifflin-St Jeor** equation (Mifflin et al., *AJCN* 1990) for REE.
   341→- Predicts REE within ±10 % of measured values.
   342→- The synthesized text does **not** print the Mifflin-St Jeor coefficients; canonical form (from the cited primary source):
   343→
   344→```
   345→REE (men)   = 10 × weight(kg) + 6.25 × height(cm) − 5 × age(yr) + 5
   346→REE (women) = 10 × weight(kg) + 6.25 × height(cm) − 5 × age(yr) − 161
   347→```
   348→
   349→**MET-based energy burn** `[bfb.txt line 58]`
   350→```
   351→CaloriesPerMinute = REE / (24 × 60) × MET
   352→```
   353→Example: REE = 1527 kcal → 1.06 kcal/min at rest; walking 4.0 mph = MET 5 → 5.3 kcal/min.

   355→### 3.8 BWP (Body Weight Planner — Kevin Hall NIH model)
   356→Source: `[fatcalc.com__bwp.txt]`
   357→- Uses **Kevin Hall PhD** mathematical body-dynamics model (NIH/NIDDK), Lancet 2011.
   358→- Replaces the "3,500 kcal = 1 lb" rule.
   359→- Models body-composition changes, BMR adaptation, glycogen/fluid shifts.
   360→- Physical Activity Level (PAL) input range: **1.4 (sedentary) to 2.3 (very active)**. Default = 1.4.
   361→- Minimum calorie floors: **1,200 kcal/day (women), 1,500 kcal/day (men)**.
   362→- IOM Acceptable Macronutrient Distribution Ranges:
   363→  - Carbs: 45–65 % of kcal
   364→  - Protein: 10–35 % of kcal
   365→  - Fat: 20–35 % of kcal
   366→
   367→(No closed-form formula in the synthesized text — Hall model is a system of differential equations. Engine action: integrate the Hall 2011 model as a numerical simulation, not a closed-form equation.)

   369→### 3.9 Target Weight Math (FatCalc BF Calculator)
   370→Source: `[fatcalc.com__bf.txt lines 76-84]`
   371→
   372→Holding lean body mass (LBM) constant:
   373→```
   374→LBM = current_weight × (1 − current_BF%)
   375→target_weight = LBM / (1 − target_BF%)
   376→```
   377→Example (180 lb @ 30 % → 20 %):
   378→- LBM = 180 × (1 − 0.30) = 126 lb
   379→- target = 126 / (1 − 0.20) = 157.5 lb
   380→- → lose ~22.5 lb of fat, keep LBM constant.

   384→## 4. Muscular Potential Models
   385→
   386→### 4.1 FFMI (Fat-Free Mass Index) — Core Formula
   387→Source: `[rippedbody.com__maximum-muscular-potential.txt lines 54, 135]`
   388→
   389→```
   390→FFMI = FFM(kg) / height(m)²
   391→where FFM = body_weight(kg) × (1 − BF%)
   392→```
   393→
   394→### 4.2 FFMI — Empirical Ceilings (Kouri et al. 1995)
   395→`[maximum-muscular-potential.txt lines 56-62]`
   396→- Study: 83 steroid users vs 74 non-users, average height ~180 cm, average BF ~13 %.
   397→- Steroid users: average FFMI ~25, max 32.
   398→- Non-users: average FFMI ~22, max 25.
   399→- **Suggested natural genetic ceiling: FFMI ≈ 25.**
   400→
   401→### 4.3 Adjusted / Normalized FFMI (height-corrected)
   402→Source: comment thread in `[rippedbody.com__maximum-muscular-potential.txt lines 1438, 1444, 1448, 1573, 1624]`
   403→
   404→Multiple commenters cite two equivalent forms. The Kouri 1995 paper version uses **6.1** (study text) vs **6.3** (abstract — likely typo). Note sign convention:
   405→
   406→```
   407→Normalized FFMI = FFMI + 6.1 × (1.8 − height(m))      [height ≤ 1.8 m → bonus]
   408→                = FFMI + 6.1 × (1.8 − height(m))      [height ≥ 1.8 m → penalty]
   409→```
   410→
   411→Equivalently ( commenter form, lines 1438 / 1624):
   412→```
   413→Adjusted FFMI = FFMI + 6 × (height(m) − 1.8)
   414→```
   415→i.e. taller people get an increment; shorter people get a decrement. (This sign is the *opposite* of the form above — there is documented confusion in the comment thread. **Engine decision:** use the Kouri 1995 canonical form `FFMI + 6.1 × (1.8 − h)` and explicitly note the ambiguity.)

   472→## 5. Cut vs Bulk Decision Tree
   473→
   474→### 5.1 Master Summary Table (RippedBody)
   475→Source: `[rippedbody.com__cut-or-bulk.txt lines 164-182]`
   476→
   477→| Category of Trainee | Recommendation |
   478→|---|---|
   479→| Overweight | **Cut** |
   480→| Underweight | **Bulk** |
   481→| Inexperienced trainee in the 13–18 % BF range | **Recomp** |
   482→| Experienced trainee over 16 % BF | **Cut** |
   483→| Experienced trainee under 16 % BF | **Cut or bulk, as per preference** |
   484→| When bulking — upper BF limit to switch to cut | **20 %** |
   485→| When cutting — lower BF limit to switch to bulk | **9–10 %** |
   486→
   487→**Add +8 % for women to every BF threshold above.**
   488→
   489→### 5.2 Operational BF Range (RippedBody)
   490→Source: `[cut-or-bulk.txt lines 92, 138, 144, 162, 182]`
   491→- **Optimal cut-bulk cycle range:** 10–20 % BF (men); 18–28 % BF (women, +8 %).
   492→- **Sweet spot:** end cut at **10 %**, end bulk at **15 %** (dedicated physique-focused clients).
   493→- **Hard upper limit:** don't exceed **20 %** BF (health risk increases past this).
   494→- **Hard lower limit (stop cutting):** **8–10 %** men, **16–18 %** women — past this, food-deprivation drives fat-storage priming.
   495→- **Minimum bulk duration:** **5 months** (don't interrupt anabolic process). Ideal bulk: 10 months before cutting.
   496→
   497→### 5.3 Recomp Eligibility
   498→Source: `[cut-or-bulk.txt lines 56-77, 84]`
   499→- **Recommended for:** relatively new trainees, OR those coming back from a long layoff.
   500→- **Definition of novice:** still making consistent **linear session-to-session progress** in training loads — *not* time-based.
   501→- **Not recommended for:** underweight (should bulk) or overweight (should cut) individuals.
   502→- **Calorie target:** maintenance (no surplus/deficit).
   503→
   504→### 5.4 Skinny-Fat Sub-Decision Tree
   505→Source: `[cut-or-bulk.txt lines 108-126]`
   506→Definition: "healthy" body weight but muscularly under-developed; weak and soft to the touch despite tensing.
   507→
   508→| Skinny-fat sub-type | Recommendation |
   509→|---|---|
   510→| Default | **Recomp** (hold calories at maintenance) |
   511→| Skinnier end of skinny-fat | Slight calorie surplus — gain ~1 % body weight per month |
   512→| Fatter end of skinny-fat | Slow cut — slightly slower than 0.5 % BW/week |
   513→
   514→### 5.5 Weight-Loss Rate Targets
   515→Source: `[cut-or-bulk.txt line 35]`, `[body-fat-guide.txt line 845]`
   516→- **Sweet spot:** ~0.5 % of body weight per week.
   517→- **Max safe rate (loose-skin avoidance):** 0.5–0.75 % BW per week.
   518→
   519→### 5.6 Obese Beginner Special Case
   520→Source: `[cut-or-bulk.txt lines 100-106]`
   521→- If client has obesity AND is starting weight training → don't necessarily start calorie counting.
   522→- Just becoming more active improves hunger signaling; BF% can drop via recomp without fat-mass loss.
   523→- Resistance training alone improves metabolic health without dieting.
   524→- Suggest behavior changes (fruits, vegetables, protein, water) before calorie tracking.
   525→
   526→### 5.7 P-Ratio Caveat (anabolic resistance myth debunked)
   527→Source: `[cut-or-bulk.txt lines 148-156]`
   528→- "Can't bulk above X % BF" myth is **incorrect** when resistance training is in place.
   529→- Nutrient partitioning in trained skeletal muscle overrides P-ratio concerns.
   530→- Counter-examples cited: sumo wrestlers (highest recorded LBM of any athlete); super-heavyweight powerlifters (stronger than lighter classes).
   531→- The 20 % BF ceiling is for **health**, not "anabolic resistance."
   532→
   533→### 5.8 Visual Estimation Reality Check
   534→Source: `[body-fat-guide.txt lines 70, 89]`
   535→- Most people **underestimate** their BF%.
   536→- Heuristic: if first-time cutting, **add 50 %** to your BF estimate → likely closer to truth.
   537→- All methods have error up to 8 % — don't track progress via BF% alone; use scale weight + tape measure.
